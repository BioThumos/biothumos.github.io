I FOUND THE CAUSE OF MY ME/CFS & LONG COVID

…or rather, the genetic vulnerability that most likely explains the onset and severity of my disease phenotype. If you’re presenting similarly to me, this might be helpful, but note that these diseases are highly individual.

Long thread, but very INFORMATION-DENSE!

First a bit of context about my symptoms and pre-disease state to understand why the revelations are so important.

My ME/CFS and Long COVID are almost exclusively in my CNS (brain & spine). I developed LC first, then a year later ME.

Before I developed PEM, I had already had strong neuro symptoms post-COVID: baseline brainfog, memory issues, cognitive slowdowns and a dull smoldering pain randomly or after mentally exerting for more than 15 minutes, helmet/forehead pressures, etc. But I could still speak, listen to music, watch movies.

With ME I got CNS PEM: brain “heaviness” & fatigue, pain & “weakness” pulsing from my nape into rest of CNS, extreme light & sound sensitivity. I also often fall asleep during crashes.

The only systemic symptom is a poisoned feeling, though this too is strongest in the CNS. I have, however, zero muscle fatigue & can walk around even during a crash.

I suspected I had autism but was never formally diagnosed. Even pre-illness, I had all sorts of sensitivities, e.g. to emotions (pain of others would distress me more tha nothers), sound, light, stress, sleep deprivation, infections, etc. Could only wear specific soft clothing, rough fabric would drive me mad. Especially despised overhead lighting and sound of vacuum cleaner.

While context switching tired me quickly, when left alone, deep concentration and usually cognitive work came easy. In fact, the problem was often that I would not be able to stop, forget to eat or other important appointments and would work into early mornings (this will be relevant later on).

All these clues suggest some kind of physiological fragility. Spoiler alert—that’s exactly what’s going on here.

If you recognize yourself in the above, you may have similar mutations as I do.

Autism itself is heterogenous & there are subtypes. To generalize, it is likely a combination of a baseline mitochondrial dysfunction (particularly complex I) & slight immune dysregulation, especially in the CNS—a chronic inflammation leading to mild immunosuppression.

Autism can be secondary to too much or too little mTOR activation causing changes to synaptic plasticity. Hypo-mTOR phenotype is usually as a result of specific pathogenic mutations, such as loss of function (LoF) of the gene SHANK3.

Indeed, it turns out I have a frame shift mutation in SHANK3, meaning there is an insert, or an extra number of base pairs that messes up the protein being produced. The exact LoF is unclear without modelling it, and SHANKS3 is a scaffolding protein, meaning it has lots of functions.

Treatment responses can also help to narrow things down.

I had an absolutely TERRIBLE response to rapamycin. A single 0.5mg pill sent me into the worst neuro-crash I ever had for 10 days straight. Immune activation shouldn’t have been that potent based on all the anecdotes I’ve seen, so we began suspecting I may have a hypo-mTOR phenotype, at least in the CNS, where the crash was taking place.

The idea is that I likely have hypo-mTOR cells in the CNS and normal or hyper-mTOR peripherally.

SHANk3 can result in hypo-mTOR and it’s often present in autism. So while the exact function loss has to be determined via computerized protein modelling, with this evidence it seems rather likely that I’m indeed hypo-mTOR.

Now, why is this relevant?

Combined with another mutation I have in NDUFS3, my mitochondrial complex I is by default inhibited and highly vulnerable to physiological stress. Think viruses, toxins, or too much energy production at once.

// insert franklin screenshot of mutation

The fact that my Long COVID phenotype was mostly CNS based, likely meant the virus or virus remnants got stuck in my brain & have been triggering ongoing immune activation and putting too much stress on my poor little mitochondria which, due to the above mutations, seem especially vulnerable to this sort of thing.

This explains why healthy people who also have high spike proteins load (more on this in another post, maybe…) but don’t develop severe symptoms like myself and others.

Besides, autists often burn themselves out or get overwhelmed because they cannot stop. They will forget to eat, sleep, or do anything other than what they’re obsessively engaged in.

This too is explained by complex I inhibition, which makes one permissive to being overstimulated or stressed out.

Indeed, during the period leading up to my LC onset, I was in non-stop stress, moving to a new flat in another city, while starting in a new role, a heart break, dealing with stressful legal things, all happening at the same time while I was working every moment I could. I was working non-stop up until that point in various high-performance jobs, not being able to stop, not even to deal with a collapsing private life.

I don’t know to what extent this buildup contributed to my illness, but a friend says that chronic illness is really the progressive accumulation of immune burden and eventually your body collapses. If you have genetic vulnerabilities, this threshold is likely a lot lower for you, like it was for me.

Still, my mitochondria had been chugging along in spite of things, without causing PEM. I have only developed that from a failed trial: An experimental NADH formula, or “cellular energy” to simplify, with a novel delivery mechanism shuttling the molecule straight into brain cells at near 100% absorption, which meant that this potent fuel put so much stress on my already over-encumbered complex I that it finally broke.

[Slightly technical, skip to (X) if not interested]

When complex I is no longer able to take electrons from reducing agents (e.g. NADH), it will start running in reverse (RET) to clear reducing equivalents. NADH builds up (already high), inhibiting the Krebs cycle & inducing more mitoROS production. This is reductive stress.

Reductive stress inhibits antioxidant enzymes & activates oxidative enzymes like NADPH oxidases, producing ROS. Enough oxidative stress damages complex III, which in a deathly spiral causes more reductive stress via FADH2 (the donor for complex III), keeping the system stuck in this metabolic trap.

Long story short, the mutations perfectly explain why this admittedly very potent NADH was the final straw, and why afterwards when I tried to rescue my NAD+/NADH levels, taking NAD+ in the same potent formula wasn’t enough to reverse the change, because by that point my complex I had already been toast.

Shouldn’t mitophagy fix this, I hear you ask?

My Amatica results show that for some other reason my mitophagic flux is nonexistent: there is pressure to replace mitochondria but it’s not being resolved for some reason. I won’t detail that here, it’s just an aside in case you were wondering.
